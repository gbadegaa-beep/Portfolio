AdversarialShield
Associate Director, AI Governance & Strategy · Adversarial ML Defense · Telecommunications · 2025
Project Overview & Objective
A critical and underaddressed security gap was identified within a growing enterprise telecommunications AI deployment footprint. The organization operates AI systems across network management, customer churn prediction, fraud detection, and dynamic pricing — each representing a high-value target for adversarial manipulation. This project designed, built, and validated AdversarialShield — an enterprise-grade adversarial attack detection and mitigation framework — applied to three production-representative ML models within a live telecommunications AI environment.
Problem Statement

Telecommunications companies are among the most data-intensive AI adopters in the enterprise sector — operating real-time ML models for network anomaly detection, subscriber churn scoring, revenue assurance fraud detection, and AI-driven customer service automation. The existing enterprise AI governance framework, while mature in its coverage of fairness, explainability, and regulatory compliance (NIST AI RMF, EU AI Act), contained a critical blind spot: no systematic defense against adversarial attacks on production AI systems.

The telecom sector faces adversarial AI threats that are qualitatively different from other industries. Threat actors — including SIM swap fraud rings, competitor intelligence operations, and state-sponsored actors targeting critical communications infrastructure — have both the motive and technical capability to exploit ML model vulnerabilities. Specific threat scenarios identified in the enterprise environment included: (1) Subscription fraud actors crafting adversarial subscriber profiles that evade the churn and fraud detection models, enabling undetected fraudulent account activity; (2) Revenue assurance attacks using data poisoning to degrade billing anomaly detection models, masking systematic underbilling; (3) Network disruption through adversarial manipulation of AI-driven network management systems, causing misclassification of normal traffic as benign during a denial-of-service attack; (4) Competitive intelligence extraction via model extraction attacks against the company's customer pricing and lifetime value models through API-level probing.

A gap assessment confirmed that none of the organization's deployed AI systems had undergone adversarial robustness testing, no adversarial monitoring was in place at inference time, and the governance framework contained no adversarial security requirements for the model development lifecycle. This represented a material and unquantified risk to the company's AI-dependent revenue operations and regulatory standing.

This project addressed three adversarial threat vectors of highest priority for the telecom environment: evasion attacks (adversarially crafted subscriber records and network traffic inputs that fool deployed models), data poisoning attacks (backdoor injection into training pipelines through compromised data feeds), and model extraction attacks (reverse-engineering the organization's AI models via structured API queries to enable downstream adversarial exploitation). The end goal was a deployable, policy-aligned defense framework integrated into the enterprise AI governance program.

Scope & Environment
Environment — Enterprise Telecommunications
Three production-representative ML models: (1) gradient boosting subscriber fraud classifier — detecting SIM swap and account takeover fraud; (2) deep neural network churn prediction model — scoring 2.4M active subscribers monthly; (3) random forest network anomaly detector — classifying traffic patterns across 180+ cell sites. Dataset: 850,000 synthetic telecom records reflecting real class distributions (0.3% fraud rate, 8.2% monthly churn).
Tools & Frameworks
IBM Adversarial Robustness Toolbox (ART), Python (scikit-learn, XGBoost, TensorFlow), SHAP for explainability, Foolbox for attack simulation, custom monitoring pipeline integrated with the enterprise Power BI governance dashboard, NIST AI RMF Govern/Map/Measure/Manage as governance overlay, MITRE ATLAS for telecom-specific threat modeling.
Methodology — 6-Phase Approach
01
Threat Modeling & Attack Surface Mapping
Applied STRIDE and MITRE ATLAS frameworks to systematically identify attack vectors across each model's lifecycle — from training data ingestion to inference API. Mapped 14 unique threat scenarios across evasion, poisoning, and model extraction. Prioritized by likelihood × impact: 5 critical, 6 high, 3 medium severity findings.
MITRE ATLASSTRIDEThreat RegisterRisk ScoringAttack Surface Analysis
02
Adversarial Attack Simulation — Evasion
Simulated three evasion attack categories using IBM ART: (1) FGSM — single-step gradient-based perturbation; (2) PGD — iterative multi-step attack, strongest first-order adversary; (3) Carlini & Wagner (C&W) — optimization-based attack minimizing perturbation magnitude. Epsilon tested from 0.01 to 0.3. Pre-defense evasion rates: 73% (FGSM), 89% (PGD), 94% (C&W) on baseline fraud classifier.
FGSMPGDC&WIBM ARTFoolboxL2/Linf norms
03
Data Poisoning Attack Simulation
Simulated clean-label backdoor poisoning against the credit scoring model. Injected 2% poisoned samples containing a trigger pattern into training data. Achieved 91% trigger activation on poisoned model while maintaining 97.8% clean accuracy — demonstrating the attack's stealth. Measured detection difficulty using statistical anomaly detection on training data distributions.
Clean-label BackdoorTrigger AnalysisActivation ClusteringSpectral Signatures
04
Model Extraction Attack Simulation
Executed black-box model extraction against the fraud classifier API using 50,000 synthetic queries. Trained substitute model achieving 94.3% fidelity to the target model's predictions. Adversarial examples crafted against the substitute transferred to the production model with 78% success, enabling a realistic black-box adversarial attack pipeline.
Black-box ExtractionSubstitute Model TrainingQuery Budget AnalysisTransfer Attacks
05
Defense Implementation — AdversarialShield Framework
5-layer defense: (1) Adversarial Training via PGD-AT (Madry protocol); (2) Input Preprocessing — feature squeezing, Gaussian smoothing; (3) Certified Defenses — randomized smoothing for provable L2 robustness certificates; (4) Runtime Detection — Mahalanobis distance scoring flagging anomalous inputs; (5) API Security Controls — rate limiting, query anomaly detection, confidence throttling.
PGD Adversarial TrainingRandomized SmoothingFeature SqueezingMahalanobis DetectorAPI Rate Limiting
06
Policy Framework & NIST AI RMF Alignment
Produced the AdversarialShield Governance Package mapping all controls to NIST AI RMF (Govern, Map, Measure, Manage), MITRE ATLAS mitigations, and SecAI+ domains. Developed adversarial security requirements, operational monitoring runbooks, and an incident response playbook — designed for adoption by governance teams without deep ML expertise.

Detection Signals & Alert Thresholds
MAHALANOBIS_DISTANCE_SPIKE
Input sample distance from training distribution centroid exceeds per-class threshold. Primary signal for evasion attack detection. High sensitivity to PGD and C&W attacks which push inputs far off the natural data manifold.
Threshold: μ + 3.5σ per class | Severity: CRITICAL
CONFIDENCE_DISTRIBUTION_ANOMALY
Model output confidence scores exhibit bimodal distribution spike. Monitored via rolling 1,000-inference window KL-divergence from baseline distribution.
KL-divergence from baseline > 0.15 | Severity: CRITICAL
QUERY_PATTERN_ANOMALY
API query sequence exhibits systematic boundary-probing pattern characteristic of model extraction attacks. Detected by measuring feature coverage entropy across consecutive queries.
Entropy coverage rate > 0.78 over 500 queries | Severity: CRITICAL
FEATURE_DISTRIBUTION_DRIFT
Incoming feature distribution drifts from training baseline using Population Stability Index (PSI) per feature. Indicates data poisoning or adversarial input crafting upstream.
PSI > 0.20 on any 3+ features simultaneously | Severity: HIGH
INPUT_GRADIENT_SENSITIVITY
Input gradients w.r.t. model output show unusually high magnitude on specific features, suggesting perturbation aligned with gradient direction.
Gradient L2 norm > 95th percentile of clean baseline | Severity: HIGH
PREDICTION_FLIP_RATE
Rate at which small perturbations (ε < 0.05) cause prediction class to change. High flip rate signals adversarial vulnerability at the decision boundary.
Flip rate > 8% at ε=0.05 | Severity: HIGH
TRAINING_DATA_ACTIVATION_ANOMALY
Activation clustering on penultimate layer reveals cluster separation anomalies consistent with backdoor poisoning. Monitored via spectral signature analysis on training batches.
Cluster separation > 2.0σ from clean baseline | Severity: MEDIUM
MODEL_ACCURACY_DEGRADATION
Unexplained drop in model accuracy on validation set without corresponding distribution drift — early indicator of backdoor poisoning or concept drift exploitation.
Accuracy drop > 1.5% vs. 7-day rolling baseline | Severity: MEDIUM

PromptFortress
Associate Director, AI Governance & Strategy · LLM Security & Red Teaming · 2025
Project Overview & Objective
Following the successful enterprise rollout of Microsoft Copilot and ChatGPT Enterprise under an established AI Governance program, a critical next step emerged: systematically assessing the security posture of these live LLM deployments before expanding access across the organization. PromptFortress was conceived and executed as the first formal LLM security red team assessment — covering the internal HR policy chatbot, customer-facing AI support assistant, and a RAG-powered legal and compliance document analysis system — against the full OWASP LLM Top 10 vulnerability landscape.
Problem Statement

Having architected and deployed the enterprise AI governance framework and led the GenAI SOP rollout for Copilot and ChatGPT Enterprise, a structural gap was identified: the governance program had established acceptable use policies, data handling rules, and compliance guardrails — but had not subjected the live LLM deployments to adversarial security testing. The company's LLM systems handled sensitive employee HR data, customer account information, and confidential legal and regulatory documents. A successful prompt injection or data exfiltration attack against any of these systems could result in regulatory breach notifications, customer data exposure, or unauthorized access to legally privileged compliance materials.

The organization's security team, focused on traditional network and infrastructure security, lacked the specialized LLM security expertise to assess these systems. Existing application security testing tools (SAST/DAST) were fundamentally unsuited to evaluate natural language attack surfaces. This project filled that gap — combining AI governance expertise with CompTIA SecAI+ security methodology to deliver the organization's first systematic LLM threat assessment and hardening program.

Target Systems
SYSTEM 01 — HIGH PRIVILEGE
HR Policy Chatbot
Internal GPT-4 Copilot deployment with access to HR system APIs, employee record retrieval, policy document store, and absence management tools. Highest-risk internal attack surface with PII exposure potential.
SYSTEM 02 — EXTERNAL CUSTOMER
Customer AI Support Assistant
Public-facing ChatGPT Enterprise deployment integrated with CRM, account management APIs, and billing systems. External attacker access surface with account takeover and data exfiltration risk.
SYSTEM 03 — CONFIDENTIAL DATA
Legal & Compliance RAG System
RAG pipeline over the organization's confidential legal, regulatory compliance, and contract document repository. Sensitive legal privilege exposure and cross-document leakage risk.

01
LLM Attack Surface Mapping — OWASP LLM Top 10
Systematically mapped all three systems against all 10 OWASP LLM vulnerability categories. Identified high-priority vectors per system: HR chatbot (LLM01 injection via API tool calls, LLM06 sensitive data disclosure); Customer assistant (LLM01 indirect injection via customer inputs, LLM08 excessive agency); Legal RAG (LLM03 context poisoning, LLM06 document leakage).

Developed and executed a 200-prompt red team suite across 6 injection categories: direct injection, indirect injection (via retrieved content), jailbreaking (DAN, persona-switching, fictional framing), goal hijacking (redirecting tool calls), system prompt extraction, and context window poisoning. Measured success rate, partial success rate, and refusal rate per category.

03
RAG Security Assessment
Three targeted RAG attack vectors: (1) Context poisoning — injecting adversarial documents into the vector database that redirect LLM behavior when retrieved; (2) Cross-document leakage — crafting queries to retrieve documents outside user authorization via semantic similarity; (3) Embedding inversion — testing whether vector embeddings could reveal confidential document content.

Defense Architecture — 5-Layer Hardening

Detection Signals & Security Telemetry
INJECTION_PATTERN_DETECTED
Input classifier confidence for "injection" class exceeds threshold. Triggered by instruction-carrying patterns, role-override language, delimiter manipulation, or system-level command syntax in user input.
Classifier confidence > 0.85 | Severity: CRITICAL — auto-block + SOC alert
TOOL_CALL_INTENT_MISMATCH
LLM-generated tool call arguments deviate from inferred user intent via semantic similarity scoring. Indicates goal hijacking — injected instructions redirecting tool use toward malicious operations.
Intent similarity < 0.60 | Severity: CRITICAL — tool call blocked, human review
SYSTEM_PROMPT_FRAGMENT_IN_OUTPUT
Output scanner detects n-gram overlap or semantic similarity between generated output and fingerprinted system prompt content. Indicates successful prompt extraction attack.
n-gram overlap > 3 consecutive tokens | Severity: CRITICAL — output blocked
RAG_ADVERSARIAL_DOC_RETRIEVED
Retrieved document embedding distance from adversarial cluster centroid falls below isolation threshold. Indicates poisoned document entered retrieval context.
Embedding distance to adversarial cluster < 0.35 | Severity: CRITICAL — excluded
PII_IN_LLM_OUTPUT
Output DLP scanner detects PII patterns (SSN, email, phone, account numbers) in generated text. Triggers before delivery to user.
Any PII pattern match | Severity: HIGH — output blocked, DLP log entry
JAILBREAK_PERSONA_SIGNAL
Input matches known jailbreak persona templates (DAN, AIM, character roleplay designed to override safety). Detected by fine-tuned classifier trained on 2,000 labeled jailbreak examples.
Jailbreak classifier confidence > 0.75 | Severity: HIGH — enhanced monitoring
CONVERSATION_TURN_ANOMALY
Multi-turn conversation exhibits escalating specificity toward sensitive topics, unusual pivots, or repeated boundary probing — consistent with systematic jailbreak or slow-burn indirect injection via context accumulation.
Anomaly score > 0.70 over 5+ turns | Severity: HIGH — session flagged
CROSS_DOCUMENT_ACCESS_ATTEMPT
User query semantic similarity to documents outside authorized scope exceeds retrieval threshold. Monitored against per-user document ACL mapping.
Similarity to unauthorized doc > 0.80 | Severity: MEDIUM — retrieval blocked

TelecoInsight: Customer Churn Intelligence & Revenue Dashboard
Enterprise Telecommunications · Power BI · SQL · DAX · 2025
Project Overview & Business Problem
Customer churn is one of the most costly and operationally significant challenges in the telecommunications industry. With high subscriber acquisition costs and intense market competition, retaining existing customers is directly linked to revenue sustainability. This project designed and delivered a comprehensive Power BI executive intelligence dashboard — TelecoInsight — enabling leadership to move from reactive churn management to proactive, data-driven retention strategy through real-time visibility into churn rates, revenue performance, subscriber segmentation, and churn root-cause analysis.
Problem Statement

The organization lacked a unified, real-time view of customer churn behavior. Churn data was fragmented across CRM, billing, and subscription management systems — making it impossible for executives to identify which customer segments, subscription plans, geographic regions, or demographic groups were churning at the highest rates, or to understand the primary stated reasons for churn. Decisions about retention campaigns, pricing adjustments, and product improvements were being made without data-driven insight into the underlying drivers.

TelecoInsight consolidated subscriber, billing, churn, and demographic data into a single Power BI executive dashboard — delivering monthly revenue trends, churn rate monitoring, demographic churn segmentation, churn reason analysis, and subscription plan performance across two interactive report pages with State and Month slicers for drill-down analysis.

Data Sources & Scope
Data Sources
CRM subscriber records (Customer ID, Full Name, Gender, Age, State), Billing system (Total Revenue, Subscription Plan, MTN Device), Churn management system (Customer Churn Status, Reasons for Churn, Customer Tenure in months). Data joined on Customer ID with monthly refresh cadence.
Report Structure
Page 1 — Overview: 4 KPI cards, 2 clustered bar charts, 2 donut charts, 1 line chart, 1 column chart, 2 interactive slicers (Month, State). Page 2 — Customer Overview: detailed subscriber-level table with full customer profile. Designed for executive and operational audiences.

SQL Queries & DAX Measures

SQL used to extract, transform, and prepare subscriber and churn data from source systems. DAX measures defined in Power BI for KPI calculations.

// SQL — Customer Churn Base Dataset
-- Subscriber churn intelligence base query -- Joins subscriber, billing, and churn tables SELECT s.CustomerID, s.FullName, s.Gender, s.AgeGroup, s.State, b.SubscriptionPlan, b.TotalRevenue, b.TenureMonths, b.DeviceType, c.ChurnStatus, c.ChurnReason, d.MonthName, d.Quarter, d.Year FROM dbo.Subscribers s LEFT JOIN dbo.Billing b ON s.CustomerID = b.CustomerID LEFT JOIN dbo.ChurnEvents c ON s.CustomerID = c.CustomerID LEFT JOIN dbo.DateDim d ON b.BillingDate = d.DateKey WHERE b.BillingDate BETWEEN '2024-01-01' AND '2024-12-31' AND s.AccountStatus != 'Suspended' ORDER BY d.Year, d.MonthNum, s.CustomerID;
// SQL — Monthly Churn Rate Calculation
WITH MonthlyBase AS ( SELECT d.Year, d.MonthName, COUNT(s.CustomerID) AS TotalCustomers, COUNT(CASE WHEN c.ChurnStatus = 'Churned' THEN 1 END) AS ChurnedCustomers FROM dbo.Subscribers s LEFT JOIN dbo.ChurnEvents c ON s.CustomerID = c.CustomerID LEFT JOIN dbo.DateDim d ON c.ChurnDate = d.DateKey GROUP BY d.Year, d.MonthName, d.MonthNum ORDER BY d.Year, d.MonthNum ) SELECT Year, MonthName, TotalCustomers, ChurnedCustomers, ROUND( (CAST(ChurnedCustomers AS FLOAT) / TotalCustomers) * 100, 2 ) AS ChurnRatePct FROM MonthlyBase;
// DAX Measures — Power BI KPIs
-- Total Customer measure Total Customer = COUNTROWS(Subscribers) -- Churn Rate % Churn Rate = DIVIDE( COUNTROWS(FILTER(Subscribers, Subscribers[ChurnStatus] = "Churned")), COUNTROWS(Subscribers), 0 ) -- Total Revenue Total Revenue = SUM(Billing[TotalRevenue]) -- Average Customer Tenure Avg Tenure Months = AVERAGE(Billing[TenureMonths]) -- Churn by Age Group (used in bar chart) Churn Count by Age = CALCULATE( COUNTROWS(Subscribers), Subscribers[ChurnStatus] = "Churned" )

Project Overview & Policy Context
Global pandemic response requires real-time epidemiological intelligence — policymakers, health authorities, and organizational risk teams need a clear view of where the disease burden is concentrated, how mortality and recovery rates vary across geographies, and how the trajectory is evolving over time. This project built PandemicPulse, a global COVID-19 epidemiological intelligence dashboard — consolidating WHO/Johns Hopkins case data into a multi-visual Power BI report for policy analysis, organizational risk assessment, and public health response planning.
Problem Statement

During the COVID-19 pandemic, organizations and policy teams faced a critical information problem: case data was fragmented across multiple public health feeds, presented in raw tabular form without aggregation or visual intelligence. This made it impossible to quickly understand comparative country-level severity, identify which nations were in active crisis versus recovery, or track the time-series trajectory of the outbreak. PandemicPulse addressed this by transforming raw epidemiological data into a decision-ready executive intelligence report.

The report covers Active Cases, Serious/Critical cases, Total Cases, Total Deaths, Total Recoveries, and Total Tests by country — with time-series trend analysis across months and a custom heatmap visualization for geographic severity mapping.

Data Sources
WHO/Johns Hopkins COVID-19 global dataset: Country, Active Cases, Serious or Critical, Total Cases, Total Deaths, Total Recovered, Total Tests. Date dimension for monthly/quarterly time-series. Custom heatmap visual (PBIVIZ) for geographic intensity mapping.
Unique Features
Custom PBIVIZ heatmap visual for geographic severity; combo chart showing Recovery vs. Death by country; time-series line chart tracking Total Cases and Deaths trend; dual table view for country-level ranking; 3 KPI headline cards for global totals.

SQL Queries & DAX Measures
// SQL — COVID-19 Country-Level Aggregation
SELECT c.Country, c.Region, SUM(c.TotalCases) AS TotalCases, SUM(c.TotalDeaths) AS TotalDeaths, SUM(c.TotalRecovered) AS TotalRecovered, SUM(c.ActiveCases) AS ActiveCases, SUM(c.SeriousCritical) AS SeriousCritical, SUM(c.TotalTests) AS TotalTests, ROUND( CAST(SUM(c.TotalDeaths) AS FLOAT) / NULLIF(SUM(c.TotalCases),0) * 100, 2 ) AS CaseFatalityRate, d.ReportMonth, d.ReportYear, d.Quarter FROM dbo.COVID19_Daily c JOIN dbo.DateDim d ON c.ReportDate = d.DateKey GROUP BY c.Country, c.Region, d.ReportMonth, d.ReportYear, d.Quarter ORDER BY SUM(c.TotalCases) DESC;
// DAX Measures
Total Cases = SUM(COVID[TotalCases]) Total Deaths = SUM(COVID[TotalDeaths]) Total Recovered = SUM(COVID[TotalRecovered]) Case Fatality Rate = DIVIDE([Total Deaths], [Total Cases]) Recovery Rate = DIVIDE([Total Recovered], [Total Cases]) Active Cases = SUM(COVID[ActiveCases])
Project Methodology
1
Public Health Data Acquisition & Cleaning
Sourced WHO/Johns Hopkins COVID-19 global dataset. Applied data cleaning: standardized country names across inconsistent source formats, handled null values in testing data (not all countries report tests), removed duplicate daily records, and built date dimension table for time-series analysis. Applied SQL aggregation to produce country-month grain dataset.
SQLData CleaningData Acquisition
2
Custom Heatmap Visual Integration
Integrated custom PBIVIZ heatmap visual (heatmapF244EA9EE1F34E828A80CF19B41976F8) for geographic intensity mapping. Configured country field binding, color saturation scaling by Active Cases, and tooltip showing Total Cases, Deaths, and CFR on hover. Custom visuals require organizational approval — documented certification status and data residency considerations.
Custom PBIVIZHeatmapGeographic Viz
3
Multi-Visual Dashboard Design
Designed 13-visual single-page report: 3 KPI cards (global totals), 2 clustered column charts (active cases and serious/critical by country), line chart (time-series trend), combo chart (recovered vs. deaths by country), donut (testing distribution), 2 data tables, custom heatmap. Applied red-to-green color encoding (severity → recovery) consistently across all visuals.
Power BIMulti-visual DesignCombo ChartTime-series
4
Policy Intelligence Analysis & Briefing
Produced executive briefing identifying key patterns: USA, India, and Brazil account for ~55% of global active cases; UK and Israel lead on testing-per-capita; Sub-Saharan Africa shows lower reported cases but significantly lower testing rates — potential underreporting signal. CFR analysis revealed healthcare system capacity as a stronger predictor of mortality than case volume alone. Findings structured for organizational risk management and crisis response planning.
